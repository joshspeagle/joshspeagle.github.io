About Me

I am a 4th-year graduate student in the Department of Astronomy at Harvard University working with Daniel Eisenstein, Charlie Conroy, Doug Finkbeiner, and Alyssa Goodman.

Research

Modern astronomy is filled with massive datasets from wide-field imaging and spectroscopic surveys containing billions of stars and galaxies. My research focuses on using a combination of astronomy, statistics, and computer science to combine and analyze these large datasets to better understand the how galaxies like the Milky Way form, behave, and evolve over time. More details can be found on my Research page.

A full list of publications can be found on ADS and arxiv.

Open Science

I am a big proponent of open, accessible, and reproducible science. When conducting research, my collaborators and I make an active effort to share all of the results, data products, and code along with interactive visualizations from our projects in order to enable readers to fully explore our findings for themselves. I also make an effort to ensure my code remains public and open-source throughout its development and is well-documented when officially released to the wider community.

Teaching

I have a strong interest in undergraduate education and pedagogy, with a focus on practical skills such as programming and statistics that are invaluable but not often taught as part of a typical science curriculum. In addition to teaching during the year and over the summer, I also try to publish pedagogical texts that can serve as useful guides for new students. I also founded and currently run the Machine Learning Journal Club at the Center for Astrophysics | Harvard & Smithsonian (CfA) to help foster discussion on many of these same topics.
